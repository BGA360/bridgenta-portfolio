## Purpose

The purpose of this constitution is to standardize how engineering work is documented and presented on BridGenta. Every project is treated not as a marketing portfolio, but as a structured engineering case study.

## Engineering Story & One Primary Question Rule

Every project must focus on telling exactly one unified engineering story. It must answer one primary engineering question:
- **BridGenta**: *How can legacy web systems be reconstructed safely using AI-assisted engineering?*
- **AEOcortex**: *How can modern AI search technologies improve discoverability?*
- **Lumina Praxis**: *How can a healthcare website combine accessibility, performance, and maintainability?*
- **Rooted Reality Gardens**: *How can a digital presence support a real business?*

## Standard Case Study Structure

Every project case study must follow this exact document structure:

### 1. Executive Summary
Brief, high-level overview of the case study, its business context, and the final outcomes.

### 2. Context
Explains what existed before the project started. Establishes the baseline system state, code condition, and organizational landscape.

### 3. Problem
Identifies the exact problem that required solving, why it was chosen, and why it mattered to the user or business.

### 4. Constraints
Outlines the operational boundaries under which the project was developed, including technical limitations, business requirements, security, privacy parameters, timelines, and resources.

### 5. Engineering Thinking
Details the methodology and structural approach. Explains how the problem was analyzed, why this strategy was chosen, and alternative paths that were considered.

### 6. Architecture
Presents the high-level system components, data flows, and design paradigms before showing code implementation details. Keep details accessible to B2–C1 readers.

### 7. Engineering Decisions
Exposes key design decisions (e.g., choice of Astro, GitHub Actions, static HTML over dynamic DB rendering, Privacy-First, or AI workflows) and documents the rationale.

### 8. Implementation
Describes how the solution was built. Avoid listing technologies; explain the specific role and purpose of each technology in the system.

### 9. Public Artifacts
Presents screenshots, layouts, or data tables as visual evidence. Each artifact must be accompanied by its purpose, the image path, and the engineering insight it illustrates.

### 10. Results
Outlines measurable outcomes in categories such as performance, maintainability, clarity, security, and workflow or documentation gains.

### 11. Lessons Learned
A reflective section discussing what worked well, what did not, and what would be improved or structured differently in a future iteration.

### 12. Future Evolution
Documents possible future directions and outlines how the project could continue to grow.

## Evidence Principle

Every engineering claim made in a case study must be backed by clear evidence, including architecture blueprints, implementation details, public artifacts, validation tests, results, and lessons learned.

---

## Screenshot & Media Philosophy

Screenshots are not decorative. Every screenshot must satisfy these three checks:
1. Why does this screenshot exist here?
2. What engineering decision does it illustrate?
3. What does the reader learn from looking at it?

---

## AI Collaboration Philosophy

Where AI builder systems contributed to a project, the case study must explicitly document:
- Exactly where AI accelerated the development process.
- Where human engineering judgment, governance, and manual code quality checks remained essential.
- The human role in final architectural decisions and code reviews.

Human accountability and review must always be made explicit.

---

## Explainability & Language Standard

- **Goal**: Complex technical concepts must become easier to understand. Every section should answer: *What? Why? How? Result? What was learned?*
- **Language**: German (B2–C1 level), professional, accessible, and technically accurate. Avoid unnecessary jargon.

## Project Quality Checklist

Before publishing or updating a project, verify:
- [ ] Does the project focus on answering exactly one engineering question?
- [ ] Is the problem clearly explained?
- [ ] Are technical, business, and timeline constraints documented?
- [ ] Is the engineering thinking and strategy visible?
- [ ] Are architectural choices explained?
- [ ] Are screenshots and media used as concrete evidence rather than decoration?
- [ ] Are technologies explained rather than just listed?
- [ ] Are results supported by outcomes?
- [ ] Does the case study teach a clear lesson?
- [ ] Are lessons learned and improvements documented?

## Closing Principle

> Every project published on BridGenta should leave the reader with a deeper understanding of both the engineering problem and the reasoning behind its solution. A successful case study demonstrates competence not by showing what was built, but by explaining why it was built that way and what was learned throughout the process.
