## 1. Overview

This document provides concise, unambiguous, and authoritative definitions for all core architectural terms utilized throughout the **Constitutional Engineering Platform (CEP)** ecosystem. Terms defined herein constitute the single source of truth (SSOT) for platform nomenclature.

---

## 2. Architectural Terminology

### 2.1 CEP (Constitutional Engineering Platform)
The operational platform that hosts, composes, and orchestrates constitutional frameworks, evidence pipelines, assessment engines, and certification registries to enforce constitutional engineering across target software projects.

### 2.2 CEF (Constitutional Engineering Framework)
The independent constitutional meta-framework that serves as the constitutional kernel inside CEP, defining meta-rules, rule evaluation semantics, assessment standards, and certification criteria.

### 2.3 Framework
A structured domain body of rules, standards, constraints, and authority boundaries (e.g., CEF, RKF, BGCF, BECC, BPGA) that defines *what* constitutional principles govern a specific engineering domain.

### 2.4 Platform
The operational substrate, infrastructure abstraction layer, and execution pipelines (CEP) that define *how* constitutional frameworks are composed, evaluated, and enforced across project lifecycles.

### 2.5 Constitution
The formal, machine-readable, and human-auditable collection of rules, principles, authority boundaries, and non-goals that governs a software project or platform.

### 2.6 Governance
The process of enforcing constitutional rules, maintaining authority boundaries, evaluating evidence, and resolving engineering compliance decisions throughout the project lifecycle.

### 2.7 Assessment
The deterministic evaluation of collected project evidence artifacts against explicit constitutional rules to produce explainable findings and compliance scores.

### 2.8 Certification
The formal process of verifying an assessment evidence chain and issuing an immutable, traceable certificate validating that a project meets a defined constitutional standard.

### 2.9 Evidence
Standardized, immutable artifacts (e.g., test outputs, static analysis logs, cryptographic hashes, metadata manifests) generated during project execution that provide verifiable proof of compliance or non-compliance.

### 2.10 Finding
A discrete, explainable outcome generated by an assessment engine that identifies a specific compliance match, warning, or violation, accompanied by its supporting evidence chain.

### 2.11 Authority
The explicit, constitutionally granted power of a framework, component, or role to render binding decisions, enforce constraints, or ownership over a specific engineering domain.

### 2.12 Responsibility
The explicit operational obligation assigned to a framework, component, or role to execute tasks, collect evidence, or produce artifacts within its authority boundary.

### 2.13 Lifecycle
The sequential stages through which a software project or platform component evolves (e.g., Stage A: Constitution Engineering, Stage B: Core Architecture, Pre-Commit, Build, Release).

### 2.14 Orchestrator
A platform component responsible for coordinating workflow state transitions, sequencing execution tasks, and aggregating data across multiple subsystems without redefining domain authority.

### 2.15 Generalization
The property of platform mechanisms that allows them to operate abstractly across diverse project domains, tech stacks, repositories, and providers without domain-specific hardcoding.

### 2.16 Reference Implementation
A concrete, fully functional implementation of an abstract specification used to demonstrate correctness, validate architectural assumptions, and provide a baseline for future adaptations.

### 2.17 Technology Independence
The design property ensuring that platform governance logic, evaluation models, and constitutional definitions remain decoupled from specific target programming languages, frameworks, or operating environments.
